Preparing Your Note for the Long Rains

Michael W. 28 February 2026 5 min read

Kenya's long rains arrive between March and May every year, and for Nissan Note owners — especially those on E11 and E12 models with lower ground clearance — being caught unprepared can mean anything from a flooded air intake to a complete electrical failure. We've put together this checklist from our in-house mechanics and 5 years of member experiences.

The 5-Point Rainy Season Checklist

1. Wiper Blades — Replace if more than 12 months old

This is the most overlooked item. Wiper blades degrade from UV exposure even when not in use. In heavy Nairobi rain, a smearing wiper is a genuine safety hazard. OEM Nissan Note wipers are available from Insight Auto. Budget: KES 800–1,500 for a pair.

2. Tyre Tread Depth — Minimum 2mm, ideally 3mm+

Worn tyres on wet roads are the leading cause of accidents in rainy season. Use a 20 shilling coin to check depth — if the silver band is visible, you're below 2mm and need replacement. The Note's 195/55 R16 (E12) and 175/65 R15 (E11) sizes are widely stocked in Nairobi. Always check tyre pressure too — cold rains can drop PSI significantly.

3. Battery and Terminals — Check for corrosion

A weak battery that starts fine in dry weather can fail completely when cold and wet. Check the terminal connections for white corrosion (clean with baking soda and warm water) and test the battery voltage — should read 12.4V+ at rest. The Note HR12 and HR15 engines are particularly sensitive to low cranking voltage in wet conditions.

4. Brake Fluid — Check level and colour

Brake fluid is hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture from the air over time. Wet brake fluid boils at a lower temperature, causing brake fade. If your fluid is dark brown rather than honey-gold, change it before the rains. DOT 3 or DOT 4 — check your reservoir cap. Service cost: KES 1,200–1,800 at most garages.

5. Air Filter — Check for water ingestion risk

This is critical for E11 owners particularly. The standard E11 air intake is positioned lower than the E12/E13. On flooded roads, water can be ingested into the engine causing hydro-lock — a catastrophic failure. Check that your air filter housing is sealed, and if you regularly drive through flooded roads, consider a raised cold air intake. Never attempt to drive through water deeper than the bottom of your doors.

Bonus: What to Keep in Your Car

  • A compact umbrella — obvious but often forgotten
  • A microfibre towel for the inside of your windscreen (condensation)
  • Jump cables or a portable jump starter (KES 3,000–6,000)
  • A torch / phone with a fully charged power bank
  • The CLUBnoteKE emergency WhatsApp number saved — fellow members have rescued more cars than any towing company

Drive safe this season. If you have additions to this list from your own experience, drop them in the WhatsApp group or the community forum — we update this guide every March based on member feedback.
